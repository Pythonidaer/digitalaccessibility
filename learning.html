Digital Accessibility Specialist

Skills

  • Critical Thinking
    • Understanding the current workflow
    • Asking where accessibility fits in from the start
    • Identifying gaps
    • Collaborating with teams to embed accessibility checks—as early as possible
    • Use guidelines, but apply them by asking the right questions
  • Organization
    • Know where to find tools and resources
    • This contributes to efficiency
    • Quicker time to action improves communication
  • Technical
    • Familiarity with Axe, WAVE, JAWS, NVDA, Acrobat, and mobile accessibility inspectors
    • Developer skills for linting, scripts, and tools such as Playwright and Storybook
  • Documentation
    • Knowing your audience
    • Ensuring guides are informative and easy to read
    • Understanding how to document in a variety of ways, such as through video tutorial, checklists, technical writing, general guides, accessibility reports, and frameworks/templates pertaining to your audience
  • Flexibility
    • To handle urgent issues outside of normal hours
      • Especially during peak academic times
    • & for emerging priorities (tackling new challenges as they arise)
  • Adaptability for evolving needs

Certifications

  • Earning the following shows you have formal training in accessibility
  • CPACC
    • The exam takes about 2 hours
    • With 100 multiple-choice questions
    • $510 for non-members ($410 for members)
  • WAS
    • Also 2 hours
    • With 75 multiple-choice questions
    • $555 for non-members ($455 for members)
  • Both are from the International Association of Accessibility Professionals (IAAP)
    • Passing both get you the CPWA certification
  • You study independently using recommended materials
    • You then register for an online proctored exam, pass it, then earn the certification

Criteria

  • Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG 2.1)

Quiz Questions

  • What is one key technical standard I'd be assessing content against as a Digital Accessibility Specialist?
    • I would be assessing content against the WCAG standards, which covers the 4 Principles of Accessibility: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust.
  • What content types would I be expected to audit and remediate for accessibility in this role?
    • I would be expected to audit and remediate for accessibility in the following content types: websites, mobile and desktop apps, video tutorials, and digital documents.
  • Legal and ethical considerations
  • What's one responsibility you'd have that involves directly supporting others—like faculty, staff, or developers—rather than just fixing issues yourself?
    • Providing accessibility consultation
      • Communicate across teams
      • Educate teams
      • Create documentation
    • Jump on phone calls with teams and individuals
    • (Sometimes) work odd hours just in case any critical accessibility issues surface
  • What types of reports or deliverables migt you be expected to produce regularly to keep stakeholders informed on accessibility progress?
    • You'd likely produce accessibility audit reports, summarizing issues found, and remidiations
    • You might also deliver progress updates, training documentation, and guidelines
    • These ensure stakeholders are kept informed on accessibility improvements and next steps
  • In this role, how would you ensure faculty & staff can continue accessible practices after your contract ends?
    • The goal is to create accessible documentation & training in multiple formats—written guides, videos, & resources that empower staff after you leave.
  • What key assistive technologies are you expected to be proficient with in this role when testing for accessibility?
    • JAWS & NVDA for screen-reader testing
    • Lighthouse, Axe & Wave for web
    • Adrobe Acrobat for PDFs
  • What's one reason the job emphasizes building accessibility into processes early, rather than retrofitting later?
    • To avoid lawsuits
    • To be proactive and not reactive
    • To work more efficiently
    • To incorporate proper accessibility guidelines & implement them
    • To have proper acessible content, whether it's website, application, video, article, or otherwise
    • By embedding accessibility early, you avoid costly fixes later & ensure a smoother, more efficient process
    • It also ensures legal compliance & equitable access from the start
  • If a faculty member asked you how to make a PDF accessible, what are two key steps you'd tell them to focus on first?
    • First, focus on using the Adobe Acrobat accessibility checker feature
    • Then, look out for things such as image alt text, form fields, & proper heading structure
  • What's one certification mentioned in the job description that shows formal accessibility expertise, and who issues it?
    • CPACC or CPWA, which is issued by the IAAP
