The Digital Accessibility Specialist role ensures compliance of the W.C.A.G. for web content.

Section 508 is part of the United States federal law (Rehabilitation Act) that mandates accessibility for federal agencies.

W.C.A.G's P.O.U.R. principles form its foundation and encapsulates the overarching requirements for accessibile digital content. Each has success criteria across multiple levels.

Types of Disabilities

Accessibiliy Laws & Guidelines address various categories of disabilities:

  1. Visual Support for blindness, low vision, and color blindness. screen reader support, text alternatives, sufficient color contrast.
  2. Hearing Support for deafness or hard of hearing. Techniques: captions, transcript visual indicators for audio cues.
  3. Motor (physical) Support for users with limited fine motor control or who cannot use a mouse. Techniques: full keyboard navigation, large click areas.
  4. Cognitive/Learning Suppot for users with dyslexia, ADHD, memory impairments. Techniques: clear layouts, consistent navigation, simple language, avoiding distractions.
  5. Neurological Support for epilepsy or other conditions triggered by flashing or motion. Techniques: avoid flashing context, provide motion reduction options.
  6. Speech Support for users who cannot use voice input. Techniques: ensure alternatives for voice-only commands.
  7. Seizure/Physical Reactions Support for users vulnerable to seizures. Techniques: no rapid flashing, pause/stop controls for animations.

These align with W.C.A.G., A.D.A., & Section 508. Each category shapes specific success criteria, ensuring digital content is inclusive.

The WCAG's Four Principles

  1. Perceivable: like text alternatives for images
  2. Operable: like keyboard navigation
  3. Understandable: clear language and predictable behavior
    • In this principle, content is clear and predictable
    • Forms should have clear instructions and error messages
    • Navigation should be consistent
    • Language should be readable
    • Users shouldn't be surprised by unexpected behavior
    • Ensure instructions are intuitive, interactions are consistent, and that users can easily understand what to do
  4. Robust: compatible with assistive technologies
  • Each principle breaks down into success criteria like color contrast or form labels
  • I'd be expected to audit content against these criteria

P.O.U.R. Elements Broken Down

This is a non-exhaustive list meant to connect examples to specific W.C.A.G. Principles

  • Perceivable
    • Images (alt text) Ensure all images have text alternatives
    • Semantic Headings Use HTML heading elements (i.e., H1, H2, etc.) to structure content logically
    • Color Contrast Ensure sufficient contrast (AA requires a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text)
    • Non-Text Content Provide alternatives for audio, video, or other media
  • Operable
    • Keyboard Navigation All functionality should be operable via keyboard
    • Focus Indicators Ensure clear focus states when navigating via keyboard
    • Consistent Navigation Repeated elements (like menus) should appear in a consistent order
    • Timing Users should have enouigh time for tasks—avoid timeouts or give options to extend time
  • Understandable
    • Forms with Labels Every form input needs clear, programmatically associated labels
    • Readable Text Content should be clear, with a reading level suitable for a wide audience, or with supplemental aids (like definitions)
    • Predictable Behavior Ensure consistent behavior, avoiding sudden changes
  • Robust
    • ARIA Labels Use ARIA attributes when native HTML isn't sufficient
    • Compatible Code Ensure content can be interpreted by assitive technologies (e.g., sreen readers, browsers, etc.)

Scope

  • (Potentially) providing input on writing
  • Collaborating with content or marketing
  • Identify readability issues for complex teams
    • Then work with content creators to simplify it
  • Provide guidance on plain language
    • But the final wording comes from those teams
  • Be a partner in the process

Content to Check

  • Websites
  • Applications (Desktop, iOS, Android)
  • Documents (PDFs, .docs, etc.)
  • Presentations (e.g., PowerPoint)
  • Video (e.g., ensuring captions are accurate)

W.C.A.G. Conformance Levels

  • A (basic)
  • AA (mid-level) Generally the level required by law or policy (like Section 508)
  • AAA (highest) More aspirational (extends further)
