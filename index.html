Accessibility in context

Accessibility is about making environments, products, and services usable by people with a wide range of abilities. This includes physical spaces such as buildings and transportation, as well as the systems people rely on in daily life.

In the United States, the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), passed in 1990, established broad protections against disability discrimination in public and commercial settings. These expectations apply to many aspects of everyday life, including access to services and information.

Later, Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act, updated in 1998, introduced more specific requirements for accessible electronic and information technology within U.S. federal agencies. As more services moved online, these expectations expanded into digital experiences such as websites, applications, documents, and media. For a fuller comparison, see Laws (ADA, Section 508).

As digital systems became central to everyday life, these expectations extended beyond physical spaces to websites, applications, documents, and media. Increased legal scrutiny and lawsuits related to inaccessible digital experiences have further accelerated awareness and adoption in recent years. For examples, see Violations.

What is digital accessibility?

Digital accessibility focuses on making websites, applications, documents, and media usable by people with a wide range of abilities, including sensory, motor, and cognitive differences.

Work in this area often involves readable content, semantic structure, compatibility with assistive technologies such as screen readers, and—when needed—explicit semantics through patterns such as ARIA.

Accessibility efforts are rarely isolated tasks. They typically involve ongoing collaboration across design, engineering, and content, with the goal of improving usability over time rather than addressing issues only once.

More detail and deeper explanations are available on the What Is Digital Accessibility page.

W.C.A.G. at a glance

Digital accessibility on the web is commonly evaluated using the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (W.C.A.G.), developed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C).

W.C.A.G. provides a shared framework for accessible content, organized into principles and testable success criteria. These are often referenced in policy at Level A or AA, depending on the required level of support.

At a high level, W.C.A.G. is structured around principles that ensure content is perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust. These principles guide how accessibility is evaluated across different types of content and interaction patterns.

For detailed explanations, examples, and conformance levels, see the W.C.A.G. Overview page.

The Digital Accessibility Specialist role

In practice, accessibility work is often guided by roles such as Digital Accessibility Specialists.

These specialists typically collaborate with design, engineering, and content teams to integrate accessibility into delivery. Responsibilities include interpreting guidelines, auditing interfaces and documents against W.C.A.G., identifying issues, suggesting remediation, and validating fixes using tools and assistive technologies.

Day-to-day work may also involve improving readability, supporting content teams, and documenting findings for stakeholders. The role focuses on enabling teams to build accessible systems, rather than owning all accessibility work directly.
